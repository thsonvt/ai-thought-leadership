Her solution: energy-based models (EBMs), which map possible outcomes onto a mathematical landscape. Likely outcomes sit in valleys, and unlikely ones sit on peaks. Whereas LLMs process one token at a time, an EBM scans the full terrain to find the lowest point, or the most probable answer. Bodnia argues that it’s this approach, not bigger LLMs, that will lead to the next AI phase shift.</p><p>Watch on <a href="https://x.com/danshipper/status/2044431229504643413" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">X</a> or <a href="https://youtu.be/Q-i8ZSUCtIc" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">YouTube</a>, or listen on <a href="https://open.spotify.com/episode/60S89CuHP2RDnG2H8rVKcF?si=K0xY56x3RTenQIEbr3iTXQ" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Spotify</a> or <a href="https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/the-ai-model-built-for-what-llms-cant-do/id1719789201?i=1000761561428" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Apple Podcasts</a>. You can also read <a href="https://every.to/podcast/transcript-the-ai-model-built-for-what-llms-can-t-do" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">the transcript</a>.</p><p>Here’s how LLMs and EBMs are different, according to Bodnia: </p><ol><li><span class="ql-ui" contenteditable="false"></span><strong>Architecture transparency:</strong> You can’t see inside an LLM; you can only evaluate its outputs. EBMs are governed by physics, which means their architecture is legible while they’re running. “Think of it as something that doesn’t play a guessing game, with an architecture that essentially allows it to self-align as it processes information,” she says. “It’s no longer a black box.” </li><li><span class="ql-ui" contenteditable="false"></span><strong>Language-based versus data-native: </strong>LLMs are language-dependent even when the task has nothing to do with language, like data analysis. “If your data is numbers, relationships, and functions, and you try to map those rules into words and then search for the next word, you’re losing a lot of information,” Bodnia says. EBMs work directly with the underlying data structure, including numbers and spatial coordinates.</li><li><span class="ql-ui" contenteditable="false"></span><strong>Sequential versus panoramic reasoning:</strong> An LLM is like navigating San Francisco without a map. Each turn constrains the next, and if you go down the wrong street, you can’t reverse course. An EBM, by contrast, has the bird’s-eye view—it can evaluate multiple routes at once and course-correct before hitting a dead end.</li></ol><p>Miss an episode?
